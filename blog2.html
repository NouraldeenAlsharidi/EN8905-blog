Why the Transition Matters Here

Regional energy needs, gas dependency, and the opportunity for offshore wind

Posted: 23 June 2026  ·  Nouraldeen M Alsharidi

Stage 1 — Regional Energy Need

Bahrain's Current Energy Landscape

Bahrain is a small island nation of 780 km² with a population of ~1.5 million and one of the highest per-capita energy consumption rates in the world. The country's electricity grid is almost entirely dependent on natural gas, which fuels Combined Cycle Gas Turbine (CCGT) and Open Cycle Gas Turbine (OCGT) power stations.

Why Gas Dominates — and Why That's a Problem

Historically, natural gas was cheap and abundant in the Gulf. However, Bahrain's gas reserves are depleting, and the country increasingly imports gas from Saudi Arabia via the GASCO pipeline. This creates an energy security risk and a growing fiscal burden on government subsidies.

Energy Security Issue: Bahrain spent approximately BD 1.2 billion on fuel subsidies in 2023. As domestic reserves decline, import dependency increases, exposing the national grid to geopolitical and price risks.

MENA Region — Offshore Wind Potential

While the Gulf is famous for solar irradiation, studies by IRENA (2023) and the World Bank identify significant offshore wind potential in the Red Sea, Gulf of Oman, and Arabian Gulf — including shallow waters around Bahrain that are technically suitable for fixed-bottom offshore turbines.

The Regional Energy Need SDG 13 Addresses

MENA is one of the most climate-vulnerable regions on Earth. According to the World Bank (2022), temperatures in the Gulf could reach 50+ °C regularly by 2050 under business-as-usual scenarios, threatening human habitability and dramatically increasing electricity demand for cooling.

Climate Risk: A 1 °C rise in average temperature increases peak electricity demand by approximately 5–8% in Gulf states (IEA). This creates a reinforcing cycle — more cooling demand → more gas burning → more warming — unless the grid is decarbonised.
Conclusion for Blog 2: Bahrain's near-total gas dependency, depleting domestic reserves, and extreme climate vulnerability make the evaluation of offshore wind as a future energy alternative directly relevant to SDG 13's regional and global targets.
